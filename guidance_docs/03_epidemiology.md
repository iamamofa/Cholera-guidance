## 3.1 Global Burden and the Seventh Pandemic

Cholera has caused **seven documented pandemics** since the early 19th century. The seventh pandemic, which began in 1961 in Indonesia, is ongoing and is characterised by the *V. cholerae* O1 El Tor biotype.

| Statistic | WHO Estimate |
|-----------|-------------|
| Annual cases | 1.3–4 million |
| Annual deaths | 21,000–143,000 |
| Countries affected | 47 (2022) |
| Underreporting factor | 5–10× estimated |

> ⚠️ These figures are widely regarded as significant underestimates due to chronic underreporting, limited diagnostic capacity, and political sensitivities around declaring cholera outbreaks.

### Key Features of the El Tor Biotype (Seventh Pandemic)

- ~75% of infections are asymptomatic, facilitating silent spread
- Greater environmental persistence than Classical biotype
- Has evolved through three distinct genomic waves (see [Page 04: Genomics](04-genomics.md))
- Currently circulating **wave 3 isolates** produce Classical-type CT (ctxB1 allele) despite El Tor genetic background

## 3.2 Geographic Distribution

### Endemic Regions
Cholera is endemic in parts of:
- **Sub-Saharan Africa** (DRC, Somalia, Ethiopia, Mozambique, Zimbabwe)
- **South Asia** (Bangladesh, India — Bay of Bengal considered the ancestral reservoir)
- **Middle East** (Yemen — ongoing catastrophic outbreak)
- **Central America** (Haiti — re-emerged 2022)

### Recent Major Outbreaks

| Country | Period | Estimated Cases | Notable Features |
|---------|--------|----------------|------------------|
| Yemen | 2016–present | >2.5 million suspected | Conflict-driven; largest in recorded history |
| Haiti | 2010–2019 | >800,000 | UN-linked introduction from South Asia |
| Haiti | 2022–present | Ongoing | Resurgence after apparent elimination |
| DRC | Endemic | Annual | Persistent; displacement-driven |
| Ethiopia | 2022–2023 | >40,000 | Drought + displacement |
| Syria | 2022–2023 | >65,000 | Conflict + infrastructure collapse |

## 3.3 Transmission Dynamics

*V. cholerae* is transmitted primarily through the **faecal-oral route**.

### Transmission Routes

```
Primary: Contaminated water
    ├── Open defecation → surface water contamination
    ├── Inadequate sewage treatment
    └── Flooding events contaminating wells

Secondary: Contaminated food
    ├── Raw/undercooked seafood
    ├── Produce irrigated with polluted water
    └── Food prepared with contaminated water

Tertiary: Person-to-person (less common)
    ├── Household transmission
    ├── Healthcare settings (poor WASH)
    └── Funeral practices (washing of deceased)
```

### Infectious Dose
- Healthy adults: 10⁶–10⁸ organisms (high dose required)
- With food as vehicle (buffering gastric acid): as low as 10² organisms
- This explains why malnutrition and antacid use increase disease risk

## 3.4 Risk Factors and Vulnerable Populations

| Risk Factor | Mechanism | Notes |
|-------------|-----------|-------|
| Malnutrition | Reduced mucosal immunity | Most important in children <5 |
| Blood group O | Higher GM1 receptor density | 2–4× increased severe disease risk |
| Gastric achlorhydria | Reduced barrier to infection | Also: proton pump inhibitor use |
| HIV/immunocompromise | Reduced adaptive immunity | Higher mortality risk |
| Pregnancy | Physiological immunomodulation | High risk of fetal loss |
| Age <5 years | Immature immunity; dehydration risk | Leading cause of childhood mortality |

## 3.5 Seasonality and Climate Drivers

Cholera exhibits pronounced **seasonality** in endemic regions:

- **Monsoon peaks:** Flooding contaminates water supplies; overwhelms sanitation
- **Dry season peaks (some regions):** Concentration around shrinking water bodies
- **Sea surface temperature (SST) anomalies:** Warm SST correlates with *V. cholerae* blooms in coastal areas

### Climate Change Projections
- Expansion of endemic transmission geographic range
- More intense transmission seasons
- Increased frequency of catastrophic flooding events
- Shifts in plankton distribution affecting environmental reservoir dynamics

## 3.6 Surveillance Systems and Case Definitions

### WHO Standard Case Definitions

| Classification | Definition |
|---------------|-----------|
| Suspected case | Person ≥5 years with acute watery diarrhoea, in area where cholera is not known to be present |
| Confirmed case | Suspected case + culture confirmation of *V. cholerae* O1 or O139 from stool |
| Probable case | Suspected case in area with confirmed cholera outbreak |

### Surveillance Pillars

1. **Sentinel surveillance** — Laboratory-confirmed surveillance at selected sites to estimate true incidence
2. **Event-based surveillance** — Rapid detection of unusual AWD clusters
3. **Genomic surveillance** — Linking WGS data to epidemiological systems for phylodynamic analysis
4. **Environmental surveillance** — Monitoring water sources for *V. cholerae* presence

> ✅ Integration of genomic surveillance with traditional epidemiological systems is a key objective of the [GTFCC Roadmap to 2030](https://www.gtfcc.org), targeting a 90% reduction in cholera deaths.

---

## 3.7 The GTFCC Roadmap to 2030

The **Global Task Force on Cholera Control (GTFCC)** Ending Cholera roadmap targets:

- **90% reduction** in cholera deaths by 2030
- Elimination of cholera transmission in **20 countries** by 2030
- Strengthening surveillance and early warning systems in all affected countries
- Increasing oral cholera vaccine (OCV) coverage in high-risk populations

Genomic surveillance is explicitly embedded in GTFCC's enhanced surveillance framework and is a strategic priority for achieving these targets.

---

## Key Takeaways

- The seventh pandemic is ongoing, driven by wave 3 *V. cholerae* O1 El Tor
- Yemen and Haiti represent the most catastrophic recent outbreaks; the DRC remains hyperendemic
- Transmission is primarily waterborne; food and household routes are secondary
- Climate change is expected to worsen the global cholera situation
- The GTFCC 2030 roadmap provides the policy framework into which genomic surveillance feeds
